Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) in Islam and Other Religions: A Comparative Perspective

Prophet Muhammad remains one of the most transformative figures in human history. While Muslims revere him as the final messenger of God, scholars and traditions across world faiths offer compelling insights into his global legacy.

Religion / Personas June 22, 2026 5 min read
HA

Written by

Hasib Ahmed Adib

Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) stands as one of the most profoundly influential figures in human history. For over two billion Muslims globally, he is the historical climax of divine revelation—the final messenger sent by Allah to re-align humanity with the principles of absolute monotheism, social justice, and systemic compassion. Beyond the borders of the Islamic world, his structural impact on civilization, law, and spirituality has made him a focal point of intense study for historians, philosophers, and theological scholars from various faith traditions.

To truly understand his legacy, one must examine both his foundational role within Islamic theology and the diverse ways he is perceived, interpreted, and acknowledged across major global religions.

Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) in Islam

In Islamic theology, Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) occupies a unique and unassailable position. He is not viewed as a divine entity or a mythological being, but as a human messenger selected by Allah for his absolute integrity to deliver the final iteration of divine law to the world.

Muslims believe that starting in 610 CE, at the age of forty, he received direct divine revelations through the Archangel Jibreel (Gabriel). These oral revelations, spaning over twenty-three years, were precisely preserved and compiled into the Holy Quran. Several core tenets define his role within the faith:

  • Lineage of Abraham: He is viewed as a direct continuation of the prophetic legacy, tracing his ancestry back to Prophet Ibrahim (Abraham) through his eldest son, Ishmael.
  • The Seal of the Prophets: Known textually as Khatam an-Nabiyyin, this doctrine affirms that Muhammad completed the long historical chain of divine revelation, meaning no new prophets or scripts will follow him.
  • The Sunnah as Blueprint: His recorded actions, explicit permissions, and legal sayings (Hadith) form the Sunnah. This serves as the primary practical template for Islamic ethics, jurisprudence, and daily lifestyle.

The cosmic scope of his mission is explicitly outlined in the sacred text itself:

"And We have not sent you except as a mercy to the worlds." (Quran 21:107)

Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) in Christianity

Mainstream Christian theology does not historically recognize Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) as an authentic prophet. Within Christian orthodoxy, divine revelation reached its final architectural fulfillment in the person and incarnation of Jesus Christ, whom Christians recognize as the Son of God.

However, interfaith scholarship reveals fascinating points of connection. For centuries, various Muslim theologians have highlighted certain Biblical texts—most notably the passages in the Gospel of John regarding the arrival of the Paraclete or "Comforter"—as early announcements of Prophet Muhammad's mission. While traditional Christian theologians uniformly interpret these passages as references to the descent of the Holy Spirit, the dialogue remains an active area of comparative analysis.

Outside of strict dogma, modern Christian historians and secular scholars widely acknowledge his monumental role in reshaping global geopolitics, preserving philosophical traditions, and advancing human civilizational frameworks.

Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) in Judaism

Similarly, classical Judaism does not accept Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) within its formal prophetic canon. Jewish theology generally maintains the historical position that the era of public prophecy concluded centuries earlier with the final prophets recorded in the Hebrew Bible (Tanakh).

Despite this theological boundary, Judaism and Islam share a massive, deeply intertwined structural heritage. Both are classified as strictly monotheistic, iconoclastic Abrahamic traditions that fundamentally reject any division of the divine unity. They share a profound reverence for common historical patriarchs and lawgivers, including Abraham, Moses, and Noah. This shared lineage manifests in remarkably similar ethical priorities: an absolute emphasis on divine law, community charity, strict dietary regulations, and social justice.

Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) in Hinduism

Because Hinduism lacks a centralized, singular ecclesiastical authority or dogmatic creed, there is no singular, official Hindu doctrine regarding Prophet Muhammad (PBUH). Views within the vast, pluralistic landscape of Hindu thought vary profoundly based on philosophical schools and localized cultural contexts.

Interestingly, certain historical cross-cultural scholars—both Muslim and Hindu—have pointed to specific verses within ancient Sanskrit texts, such as the Bhavishya Purana, suggesting they contain symbolic prophecies pointing to a spiritual teacher arriving from the arid regions of Arabia. While these specific interpretations are highly debated, non-traditional, and not universally held within mainstream Hindu orthodoxy, they represent early historical attempts to construct conceptual bridges between the two major demographics.

Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) in Buddhism

Mainstream Buddhist traditions do not recognize Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) as a Buddha or an enlightened teacher within their internal framework, as Buddhism focuses heavily on the psychological and philosophical insights of Siddhartha Gautama rather than a line of monotheistic prophets.

Nevertheless, comparative religion researchers have often explored intriguing conceptual parallels. Some point to early texts detailing the Maitreya—a future, fully awakened Buddha who will appear on Earth to restore the dharma and teach pure compassion. While orthodox Buddhists maintain that this figure operates within an entirely separate cosmological cycle, comparative scholars note that the universal virtues attributed to the arriving figure echo the global ethical renewal brought by the Prophet of Islam.

A Lasting Global Legacy

Regardless of shifting theological perspectives or personal faith alignments, objective secular history recognizes Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) as an unmatched force in human social engineering. In a brief span of twenty-three years, his leadership completely transformed the fractured, tribal landscape of the Arabian Peninsula into a highly sophisticated, unified civilization.

His systemic insistence on critical humanitarian tenets—including the mandatory distribution of wealth through charity, the codification of women's property rights, the abolition of tribal blood feuds, and an absolute rejection of racial hierarchy—laid the legal groundwork for an empire that would soon stretch from Spain to India. Today, his specific guidelines continue to direct the intimate internal lives and moral compass of over 1.9 billion people across the planet.

Conclusion

Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) remains a monumental anchor of world history. Within Islam, he is the ultimate lens through which humanity understands the divine will. For other faiths, he is an ideological pioneer whose civilizational blueprint fundamentally redirected the course of human development. Investigating how different traditions analyze his life does not require a compromise of personal belief; rather, it offers a vital path toward mutual respect, historical clarity, and nuanced dialogue in an interconnected world.
