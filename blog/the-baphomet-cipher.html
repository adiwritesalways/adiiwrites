The Baphomet Cipher: How a Dead Language Code May Have Hidden the Templars' Greatest Secret

How a respected New Testament scholar used an ancient Hebrew code to uncover the potential secret behind the Knights Templar's legendary idol.

History May 24, 2026 6 min read
HA

Written by

Hasib Ahmed Adib

Few names in the history of Western esotericism carry as much weight — or as much misunderstanding — as Baphomet. Invoked by inquisitors, adopted by occultists, painted onto tarot cards, and eventually sculpted into one of the most recognizable images in modern occult iconography, Baphomet has meant many things to many people across seven centuries. But one of the most quietly revolutionary theories about the name's origin came not from a mystic or a conspiracy theorist, but from a respected New Testament scholar named Hugh Schonfield — and it came wrapped in the elegant machinery of an ancient Hebrew code.

The Accusations and the Name That Haunted the Templars

To understand the Baphomet mystery, you have to go back to those mass arrests of October 1307 — the same Friday the 13th that shattered the Knights Templar. Under torture, Templar knights across France began confessing to extraordinary crimes: spitting on the cross during initiation, engaging in obscene kisses, worshipping an idol called Baphomet.

The confessions were almost certainly coerced. Men on the rack will say anything. But the name itself was peculiar — peculiar enough that it survived long after the Templars were dust. Where had it come from? What did it mean?

The most obvious interpretation — and the one medieval inquisitors favored — was that Baphomet was a corruption of Mahomet, the Old French rendering of Muhammad. The implication was clear and politically useful: the Templars, who had spent generations in the Holy Land in proximity to Islamic culture, had secretly converted to the worship of the Prophet. It was a charge designed to blacken them beyond redemption in the eyes of Christian Europe.

But this explanation never quite satisfied. It was too convenient, too perfectly suited to the needs of a king who wanted justification for destroying the order. And it left a nagging question: if the Templars had simply wanted a code name for Muhammad, why choose a word so strange, so unlike any known rendering of that name?

Hugh Schonfield: The Scholar Who Looked at Letters

Hugh Schonfield was not an occultist. Born in 1901 in London, he was a serious biblical scholar, a lifelong student of early Christianity and Jewish religious thought, and the author of The Passover Plot — a controversial 1965 book arguing that Jesus had deliberately orchestrated the circumstances of his death and resurrection. Whether you agreed with him or not, Schonfield was a rigorous thinker who worked close to primary sources and took ancient languages with full seriousness.

It was this linguistic seriousness that led him, in his 1984 work The Essene Odyssey, to look at the word Baphomet not as a theological puzzle but as a cryptographic one. And what he applied to it was one of the oldest known ciphers in human history: the Atbash cipher.

The Atbash Cipher: Reversing the Alphabet

The Atbash cipher is breathtaking in its simplicity. It is what cryptographers call a substitution cipher — a system where each letter of an alphabet is replaced by another letter according to a fixed rule. In Atbash, the rule is total reversal: the first letter of the alphabet becomes the last, the second becomes the second-to-last, and so on all the way through.

In Hebrew, where Atbash originates, this means:

Aleph (א), the first letter, becomes Tav (ת), the last
Bet (ב), the second letter, becomes Shin (ש), the second-to-last
Gimel (ג) becomes Resh (ר)
And so on, mirror-image, through the entire alphabet

The name itself encodes the system. A-T-B-Sh — Aleph-Tav, Bet-Shin — the first two substitution pairs, collapsed into a single word. It is a cipher that announces itself in its own name.

Atbash is ancient. It appears in the Hebrew Bible itself — in the Book of Jeremiah, where the word Sheshach appears as a name for Babylon. Biblical scholars long puzzled over this, since Sheshach appears nowhere else as a name for that city. When you apply Atbash, the mystery resolves immediately: Shin-Shin-Kaf becomes Bet-Bet-Lamed — Babel. Babylon, encoded. The prophet hid a name in plain sight using a cipher his literate readers would have recognized.

Atbash also appears in Jeremiah's rendering of Lev Kamai — "the heart of those who rise against me" — which decodes to Kasdim: the Chaldeans. These were not decorative puzzles. They were a way of speaking truth while maintaining plausible deniability — encoding danger in the language of riddles.

Schonfield's Revelation: Baphomet Decoded

Schonfield's insight was to ask a deceptively simple question: what if you wrote the word Baphomet in Hebrew letters, and then ran it through the Atbash cipher?

The name Baphomet, rendered phonetically into Hebrew script, gives you five letters: Bet — Pe — Vav — Mem — Tav (ב-פ-ו-ม-ת).

Now apply Atbash — replace each letter with its mirror opposite across the Hebrew alphabet:

Bet (ב) → Shin (ש)
Pe (פ) → Ayin (ע)
Vav (ו) → He (ה)
Mem (מ) → Yod (י)
Tav (ת) → Aleph (א)

The result: Shin — Ayin — He — Yod — Aleph.

Read those five Hebrew letters together and you get: Sophia.

Sophia: The Hidden Goddess of Wisdom

Sophia is the Greek word for wisdom — but in the theological traditions that flourished in the Near East during and after the time of the Templars, she was far more than a translation. Sophia was a figure, a divine principle, in many cases a goddess: the feminine face of divine wisdom, present at the creation of the world, the companion of God before the foundation of the earth.

She appears in the Hebrew Bible in the Book of Proverbs, where Wisdom is personified as a woman who was with God from the beginning, crying out in the streets, inviting all who will listen to her feast. She becomes more elaborate in the deuterocanonical Book of Wisdom and in the Wisdom of Sirach. And in the Gnostic traditions that ran parallel to early Christianity, Sophia became a central cosmic figure — the divine emanation whose fall from the pleroma, the divine fullness, was said to have brought about the material world.

The Gnostics revered her. Some early Christian movements bordering on Gnosticism honored her. And the Knights Templar, who had spent generations in the syncretistic, intellectually fertile environment of the Crusader Holy Land — absorbing influences from Jewish Kabbalism, Islamic philosophy, and surviving strands of Gnostic thought — may have quietly venerated her too.

If Schonfield's decipherment is correct, then Baphomet was never an idol, never a demonic goat-head, never a corruption of Muhammad's name. It was a hidden word. A secret written in letters, pointing to the feminine divine wisdom that certain members of the order may have privately honored — a reverence they could not safely confess in a world where such things meant the stake.

Why the Templars Would Have Known Atbash

This is where Schonfield's theory achieves a kind of elegant plausibility. The Knights Templar were headquartered for much of their early existence on the Temple Mount in Jerusalem — the site of Solomon's Temple, the holiest ground in Judaism. They lived in daily proximity to Jewish scholars, merchants, and Kabbalists. They learned Arabic. They absorbed the intellectual currents of a world far more cosmopolitan than the Europe they had left behind.

The Atbash cipher was not obscure among those who studied Hebrew scripture seriously. Any Templar knight with a serious interest in the Hebrew Bible — or in the mystical traditions growing up around it — would have had access to this tool. It was hiding in Jeremiah, waiting to be found.

To encode a sacred name in Atbash would have been a sophisticated act, one that required both knowledge of Hebrew and familiarity with the cipher. It was the kind of thing a learned inner circle might do — protecting a dangerous piece of theology in a word that sounded, to the uninitiated, like meaningless noise.

The Lévi Baphomet: When the Image Was Invented

It is worth pausing to note that the iconic image of Baphomet — the winged, goat-headed, hermaphroditic figure seated in a pentagram — has a surprisingly recent origin. It was created in 1856 by the French occultist Éliphas Lévi for his book Transcendental Magic. Lévi called this figure the Sabbatic Goat, the Baphomet of Mendes, and loaded it with layers of hermetic symbolism: the torch of illumination between the horns, the caduceus in the lap, one arm pointing up and one down in the gesture as above, so below, the crescent moons, the scales.

It is a magnificent piece of symbolic art. But it is Lévi's invention, not a Templar artifact. The Templars never worshipped or depicted anything resembling it — the image was constructed from the occult imagination of nineteenth-century France, centuries after the last Templar was dead.

This matters because it means Baphomet the word and Baphomet the image are almost entirely separate traditions that later collapsed into one. The word came from the Templar accusations of 1307. The image came from Lévi's drafting table in 1856. The merger of the two — the assumption that what the Templars worshipped looked like Lévi's goat-figure — is a historical error so widely repeated it has become its own kind of mythology.

If the Templars were indeed hiding Sophia behind the Baphomet cipher, then what they were concealing was not a demonic idol at all, but a feminine, philosophical, almost contemplative religious devotion — closer to mystical Judaism or Gnostic Christianity than to anything Satanic.

Objections and the Limits of the Theory

Schonfield's theory is compelling, but honesty demands that its limitations be acknowledged.

The most serious objection is the language question. The Templars were French, Occitan, and Norman knights. Their written records were in Latin and Old French. There is no direct evidence that the Templar leadership had Hebrew literacy sufficient to construct or use an Atbash cipher — this remains an inference, however plausible, based on their proximity to Jewish culture in the Holy Land.

There is also the fundamental uncertainty about the confessions themselves. We do not know whether Baphomet referred to a real object of veneration within the order, a ritual element known only to the inner circle, a word borrowed and misunderstood, or simply a name invented under torture by men trying desperately to give their inquisitors something. The confessions are corrupted at the source.

Scholars remain divided. Some find Schonfield's Atbash solution elegant to the point of being convincing. Others consider it too neat — the kind of perfectly fitting answer that should make a careful historian suspicious precisely because it satisfies so completely. History rarely resolves its mysteries this tidily.

What the Theory Means

Regardless of whether Schonfield was right in every particular, his contribution to the Baphomet question was genuinely important. He shifted the conversation from demonology to linguistics. He asked not what evil thing does this name represent but what does this name actually say — and in doing so, he opened a window onto a very different possibility.

A possibility in which the Knights Templar, far from being devil-worshippers, were sophisticated theological explorers who had absorbed from their long sojourn in the East a reverence for Wisdom — for Sophia, the feminine principle that orthodox Christianity had largely suppressed. A possibility in which the name Baphomet was not a blasphemy but a secret: a hidden confession of faith encoded in a cipher old enough to appear in the pages of the Hebrew prophets, hidden in plain sight within a word that their enemies would never think to decode.

The Atbash cipher works. The substitution is exact. The name that emerges — Sophia — is theologically coherent with everything we know about the intellectual world the Templars inhabited.

Whether it is the truth, or a beautiful coincidence shaped into meaning by the pattern-hungry human mind, may never be known with certainty.

But the letters remain. Reversed, reflected, whispering the name of a hidden goddess across seven centuries of silence.

The history of the Templars is the history of what powerful institutions do to ideas they cannot control — they name them evil, burn their guardians, and scatter the ashes. But some ideas survive in the shape of words. And some words, read backwards, say something entirely different.
