How Friday the 13th Became an Evil Day: A History of the World's Most Famous Superstition

Discover the dark origins of Friday the 13th – from the Knights Templar massacre and Biblical betrayal to ancient myths – and how it became the world’s most feared unlucky day

History May 24, 2026 7 min read
HA

Written by

Hasib Ahmed Adib

Every month that begins on a Sunday contains a Friday the 13th. Most people walk into it with at least a faint unease — avoiding ladders a little more carefully, hesitating before signing contracts, perhaps secretly relieved when the day passes without incident. But where did this dread come from? The answer is not a single dramatic origin story. It is a slow, centuries-long accumulation of darkness, drawing from theology, mythology, medieval history, and the deep human instinct to find pattern in misfortune.

Thirteen: The Number That Broke the Circle

Long before Friday entered the picture, the number thirteen was already suspect.

The unease likely begins with something deeply intuitive: twelve is a number of completeness. Twelve months in the year. Twelve hours on a clock face. Twelve signs of the zodiac. Twelve tribes of Israel. Twelve apostles. Twelve gods of Olympus. Twelve is the number of a finished, harmonious system. Thirteen, by contrast, is the thing that shows up uninvited — the one extra guest that upsets the symmetry.

In Norse mythology, this anxiety takes vivid form. The gods of Asgard were holding a banquet — twelve of them, a perfect assembly — when Loki, the trickster, arrived uninvited as the thirteenth guest. Through his scheming at that feast, the beloved god Balder was killed, plunging the world into grief and setting in motion the events that would eventually lead to Ragnarök. The thirteenth seat at the table was the seat of chaos.

The Last Supper carried a parallel weight in Christian Europe. Thirteen gathered in the upper room: Jesus and his twelve disciples. The thirteenth to sit was Judas Iscariot, the betrayer. By morning, Jesus was on his way to crucifixion. The association was seared into the European imagination — thirteen at a table meant one would die before the year was out.

This superstition lasted centuries. Well into the nineteenth century, formal dinner parties in Paris could hire a quatorzième — a professional fourteenth guest — to prevent the disaster of a table set for thirteen.

Friday: The Day With a Dark Reputation

Friday arrived at its evil reputation through an entirely separate tradition, and its roots run just as deep.

In the Christian world, Friday was the day of the Crucifixion. It was a day of fasting, mourning, and penitence. Beginning important ventures on a Friday was considered courting disaster — you were starting something on the day the world's greatest tragedy unfolded.

Popular tradition heaped further misfortune onto the day. It was said that Adam and Eve ate the forbidden fruit on a Friday. That Cain killed Abel on a Friday. That Noah's flood began on a Friday. That the Tower of Babel collapsed on a Friday. Whether any of these claims had scriptural basis mattered less than the cumulative weight they carried. Friday became a day on which things went wrong — a day shadowed by the fall of humanity itself.

In maritime culture, Friday was particularly dreaded. Sailors refused to begin a voyage on a Friday, believing it invited shipwreck. There is a famous — almost certainly apocryphal — story of the British Navy attempting to break this superstition in the nineteenth century by laying the keel of a new ship on a Friday, launching it on a Friday, naming it HMS Friday, and sending it out under a Captain Friday. The ship was never seen again. Whether the story is true or not speaks to how deeply the belief had lodged itself.

The Medieval Catastrophe: Knights Templar and Friday the 13th

Here is where history and legend become almost impossible to separate — and where the specific combination of Friday and thirteen acquires its most dramatic origin story.

On Friday, October 13, 1307, King Philip IV of France ordered the mass arrest of the Knights Templar. The Templars were the most powerful military-religious order in the medieval world — warrior monks who had fought in the Crusades, accumulated vast wealth, and operated a kind of proto-banking network across Europe. Philip, deeply in debt to the order and coveting their riches, convinced Pope Clement V to move against them.

In a single coordinated sweep, hundreds of Templars were arrested simultaneously across France. Under torture, many confessed to heresy, blasphemy, and obscene rituals — confessions almost certainly extracted by force. Their leaders, including Grand Master Jacques de Molay, were eventually burned at the stake. The order was dissolved. Their wealth was seized.

The date — Friday the 13th — was not a coincidence. Philip chose it deliberately, perhaps for its already ominous associations, perhaps to reinforce the sense that the Templars were cursed, illegitimate, deserving of their fate.

Whether this event created the modern superstition or merely reinforced an existing dread is debated by historians. Some argue the Templar story has been over-romanticized and that its connection to our modern Friday the 13th anxiety is largely the invention of later storytellers. But the fact remains: one of the most catastrophic single days in medieval history fell on a Friday the 13th, and the story has never been forgotten.

The Nineteenth Century: When the Superstition Crystalized

Remarkably, despite all this accumulated folklore, the specific phrase Friday the 13th — the two anxieties fused into one — does not appear clearly in the historical record until the nineteenth century.

Before then, the two superstitions ran on parallel tracks. Thirteen was unlucky. Friday was unlucky. But the combination as a distinct, named fear appears to solidify in popular culture only in the 1800s, as literacy spread and newspapers and almanacs began cataloguing folk beliefs for a mass readership.

An 1869 biography of the composer Gioachino Rossini noted that he considered Friday an unlucky day and thirteen an unlucky number — and that, as if fate had arranged the cruelest possible joke, he died on Friday, November 13th. The anecdote spread widely, cementing the combination in the popular imagination.

By the early twentieth century, the fusion was complete. In 1907, Thomas Lawson published a novel called Friday, the Thirteenth, about a Wall Street manipulator who exploits the superstition to crash the stock market. It was a bestseller. The date had become a cultural shorthand for impending doom.

The Psychology Behind the Fear

Why does this particular superstition persist when so many others have faded? The answer lies partly in how the human mind processes risk and coincidence.

WWe are pattern-seeking creatures. When something goes wrong on a Friday the 13th, we remember it — it confirms what we already suspected. When nothing goes wrong, the day simply passes. This confirmation bias means the superstition perpetually refreshes itself through selective memory.

There is also the self-fulfilling dimension. People who are anxious drive more cautiously, avoid risky decisions, stay home. When they do venture out, they are hypervigilant. Paradoxically, studies have suggested that fewer accidents may occur on Friday the 13th in some contexts, precisely because people are paying closer attention than usual.

The formal name for the fear of Friday the 13th is paraskevidekatriaphobia — from the Greek for Friday (Paraskevi), thirteen (dekatria), and fear (phobia). It is a word so unwieldy it almost disproves the fear by making people laugh. But the condition is real enough that economists have estimated it costs the United States economy over a billion dollars annually in lost productivity, cancelled flights, and avoided transactions.

Popular Culture and the Locked-In Legend

If any single moment sealed the superstition in the modern imagination, it was the 1980 release of Friday the 13th — a low-budget horror film that became a cultural phenomenon and spawned one of the most recognizable franchises in cinema history. The hockey-masked killer Jason Voorhees became the visual embodiment of what the date had always implied: something waiting in the shadows, patient, inevitable, arriving on schedule.

It is one of popular culture's great feedback loops. The film used the superstition; the superstition was amplified by the film; generations grew up for whom the two were inseparable.

The Living Superstition

What makes Friday the 13th so fascinating as a cultural phenomenon is that it is simultaneously ancient and modern, theological and secular, deeply felt and gently mocked. People who would laugh at the idea of genuinely fearing the number thirteen will still pause, just slightly, when the date arrives. It operates somewhere between belief and habit — a ghost story we tell ourselves so often it starts to feel like memory.

The superstition survives because it is a vessel. It holds the medieval terror of Templar arrests, the solemn grief of Good Friday, the instinctive unease of that uninvited thirteenth guest. It holds every unanswered question about why bad things happen on specific days, and whether the universe is indifferent or quietly malevolent.

And so the calendar turns, and the day arrives, and somewhere a cup is left unfilled on a table set for twelve — just in case — and the old dread stirs again, wearing its ancient face in a modern world that insists it knows better.

We always did love to be a little afraid.
