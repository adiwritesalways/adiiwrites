The Brotherhood of the Square and Compass: A Complete Guide to Freemasonry

Explore the true history, rituals, and philosophy of Freemasonry, separating the historical truths of medieval stonemasons from the legendary myths.

History/Philosophy May 25, 2026 12 min read
HA

Written by

Hasib Ahmed Adib

There is a handshake you are not supposed to know about. There are words, gestures, and passwords exchanged in rooms you are not supposed to enter. There are aprons worn by judges, generals, presidents, and kings — men who, in the outside world, would never stand as equals, but inside these walls address each other simply as brothers. There are allegories performed in candlelit chambers, drawing on the tools of medieval stonemasons to teach lessons about morality, death, and the architecture of the soul.

This is Freemasonry. And despite being one of the oldest, largest, and most documented fraternal organizations in human history, it remains genuinely mysterious — not because its secrets are so explosive, but because the gap between what people imagine it to be and what it actually is has never fully closed.

This is an attempt to close that gap — or at least to narrow it considerably.

Where Did It Come From? The Origins Debate

The question of Freemasonry's origins is one that Masons themselves have debated for centuries, and it is worth separating the legendary origins from the historical origins, because both matter — one as mythology, one as fact.

The Legendary Origins

Masonic tradition, as taught within the lodges, traces the fraternity back to the building of King Solomon's Temple in Jerusalem, around 950 BCE. According to this legend, Solomon engaged the services of a master architect named Hiram Abiff, sent by the King of Tyre, to oversee the temple's construction. Hiram Abiff possessed the secret passwords and signs of the master craftsman — the highest trade secrets of the ancient building guild. Three jealous workers, wanting those secrets, confronted Hiram and demanded he reveal them. When he refused, they murdered him.

This story — the Legend of Hiram Abiff — is not just historical background in Freemasonry. It is a ritual drama, re-enacted ceremonially during the initiation into the Third Degree, the degree of Master Mason. The candidate plays the role of Hiram. He is symbolically murdered, symbolically buried, and symbolically raised from the dead by the grip of the Master Mason — a ritual that carries profound themes of death, resurrection, and the triumph of integrity over cowardice.

Masons also claim connections, through various traditions, to the Knights Templar, the ancient mystery schools of Egypt and Greece, the Rosicrucians, and even to Noah's Ark (since a master builder would have been needed for such a vessel). These connections are almost entirely symbolic and allegorical rather than historical. They speak to the Masonic love of ancient wisdom and continuity with the great builders of civilization — but they are not to be taken as literal genealogy.

The Historical Origins

Historically, Freemasonry as an organized institution traces back to the medieval stonemason guilds of Europe — the actual working craftsmen who built the great cathedrals, castles, and public buildings of the Middle Ages. These guilds had genuine trade secrets: the mathematical knowledge required to calculate load-bearing arches, the tricks of cutting and finishing stone, the engineering methods behind soaring Gothic vaults. In an era before professional licensing, these secrets were the guild's competitive advantage, and they were protected fiercely.

Guild members had passwords, signs, and tokens to identify each other when traveling — a way of proving to a master builder in a foreign city that you were a genuine trained craftsman and not an impostor. They had lodges — physical structures on building sites where workers ate, slept, stored tools, and met. They had degrees of rank: Entered Apprentice, Fellow of the Craft, and Master Mason — the same three degrees that survive in Freemasonry today.

As the great cathedral-building era wound down in the 17th century and the demand for operative (working) stonemasons declined, these guilds began admitting non-operative members — educated gentlemen, philosophers, and aristocrats who were attracted to the guild's traditions, symbols, and fellowship, but who had no intention of actually cutting stone. This transition from operative to speculative Masonry — using the tools and language of building as metaphors for moral and philosophical self-construction — is the key hinge point in Freemasonry's history.

1717: The Grand Lodge of London

The conventional founding date of modern, organized Freemasonry is June 24, 1717, when four London lodges came together at the Goose and Gridiron Alehouse in St. Paul's Churchyard to form the Grand Lodge of England — the first grand lodge in the world and the organizational model for all that followed. They elected a Grand Master, established a governing structure, and began the process of standardizing Masonic ritual and practice.

In 1723, the Scottish Presbyterian minister James Anderson compiled the Book of Constitutions — the foundational document of modern Freemasonry. It laid out the rules, history (including the legendary origins), and moral framework of the fraternity. One of its most remarkable features was its approach to religion: Masons were required to believe in a God — referred to as the Grand Architect of the Universe — but the lodge was explicitly non-sectarian. Catholics, Protestants, Jews, and eventually men of any faith could sit as brothers. In an era of ferocious religious warfare, this was a radical position.

From London, Freemasonry spread with remarkable speed. By the mid-18th century, lodges existed across Britain, France, the German states, the American colonies, and beyond. The fraternity traveled with merchants, soldiers, diplomats, and scholars, planting itself wherever educated men gathered.

What Actually Happens Inside a Lodge?

This is the question most non-Masons most want answered, and the answer is simultaneously more mundane and more interesting than the imagination typically produces.

A Masonic lodge is a chartered organization that meets regularly — typically once or twice a month — in a dedicated building or rented hall called a lodge room. The room is arranged symbolically: an altar at the center bearing the Volume of Sacred Law (usually a Bible, but adapted to the faith of the membership), the Square and Compasses — the iconic symbol of Freemasonry — placed upon it. Officers sit at specific stations around the room, each with a title borrowed from ancient guild tradition: the Worshipful Master presides from the East (symbolizing the rising sun and the source of light and knowledge); the Senior Warden sits in the West; the Junior Warden in the South.

Members wear aprons — a direct inheritance from the working stonemason, for whom the leather apron was a practical tool of the trade. In speculative Masonry, the apron is purely symbolic and ceremonial, representing the purity of character a Mason aspires to. The aprons of higher-ranking officers are elaborately decorated with symbols.

A typical lodge meeting involves:

  • Opening ceremonies — a formal, ritualized opening of the lodge, performed in a specific degree, with officers exchanging set words and gestures that have remained largely unchanged for centuries.
  • Business — minutes, finances, membership votes, charitable work, administrative matters. Masonry is, among other things, a working organization with real-world responsibilities.
  • Degree work — the initiation or advancement of candidates through the three degrees. This is the heart of Freemasonry's ceremonial life. Each degree confers new knowledge, new obligations (sworn on the Volume of Sacred Law), and new symbolic understanding.
  • Closing ceremonies — a formal closing, mirroring the opening.

After the formal meeting, most lodges adjourn to a festive board — a shared meal with toasts, fellowship, and the kind of easy brotherhood that is, frankly, the feature most Masons say they value most.

The Three Degrees: A Journey in Symbols

The three degrees of the Blue Lodge (the foundational lodge, so named for the blue decor traditionally used) are the core of Masonic experience. Every Mason, however far he goes in the broader Masonic system, passes through these three.

First Degree: Entered Apprentice

The candidate for initiation is prepared in a way that has unnerved and moved men in equal measure for centuries. He is divested of all metal — no coins, no watch, no jewelry — symbolizing that wealth and worldly status mean nothing at the threshold of the lodge. He is hoodwinked — blindfolded — symbolizing his current state of darkness and ignorance. One trouser leg is rolled up, one sleeve rolled up, one shoe replaced with a slipper — in a state of curious asymmetry that has ritual meaning debated across generations.

He is led to the door of the lodge and caused to knock. He is challenged. He declares himself a man freely seeking the light of Freemasonry. He is led inside, the hoodwink is removed, and he sees the lodge — lit, arranged, and populated with brothers — for the first time.

He is taught the Working Tools of the Entered Apprentice: the twenty-four inch gauge (representing the division of the day into work, rest, and service to God and a distressed brother) and the common gavel (representing the force of conscience, chipping away at the rough edges of character). He swears an obligation of secrecy and brotherhood. He is taught a password and a grip. He is now, in Masonic terms, a rough ashlar — an unfinished stone, awaiting the work of moral and intellectual refinement.

Second Degree: Fellow Craft

The second degree deepens the philosophical content considerably. The Fellow Craft is introduced to the liberal arts and sciences — grammar, rhetoric, logic, arithmetic, geometry, music, and astronomy — as the tools of a fully developed human intellect. He passes between two great symbolic pillars (representing Jachin and Boaz, the pillars of Solomon's Temple) and ascends a symbolic staircase.

The working tools of this degree are the square (morality), the level (equality), and the plumb (uprightness). These are not just tools — they are a complete ethical system expressed in the language of construction.

Third Degree: Master Mason

The third degree is the most dramatic and the most profound. It centers entirely on the Legend of Hiram Abiff — the murder, concealment, and symbolic raising of the master architect. The candidate plays Hiram. He is symbolically slain, laid in a symbolic grave, and raised by the Lion's Paw Grip of the Master Mason.

The lesson is transparent and ancient: integrity unto death. Hiram possessed secrets he refused to reveal under pain of death. He died rather than betray his trust. In a world that constantly pressures people to compromise their principles for survival or advantage, this is the fraternity's central moral teaching: there are some things worth dying for, and your word is one of them.

Once raised as a Master Mason, a man is considered a full member of the brotherhood. Everything that follows — the higher degrees, the appendant bodies, the further mysteries — is built on this foundation.

The Higher Degrees: York Rite, Scottish Rite, and Beyond

Many people are surprised to learn that the three degrees of the Blue Lodge are, in a sense, just the beginning. Freemasonry has developed an elaborate ecosystem of appendant bodies offering additional degrees, each with their own rituals, histories, and philosophical content.

The two major systems in the Anglophone world are:

The York Rite

The York Rite confers degrees through three bodies: the Chapter of Royal Arch Masons, the Council of Cryptic Masons, and the Commandery of Knights Templar. The Royal Arch degree is considered by many Masons to complete the Master Mason degree — it claims to reveal the full secret that Hiram died protecting. The Commandery brings Masonry into explicitly Christian territory, with degrees drawing on the imagery and history of the medieval crusading orders.

The Scottish Rite

Despite the name, the Scottish Rite was largely developed in France and formalized in America in the early 19th century. It confers degrees numbered 4 through 32, with an honorary 33rd degree awarded for exceptional service to Masonry. Each degree is a philosophical play or lecture on a theme — justice, truth, charity, the nature of God — drawing on a vast range of sources: Kabbalah, ancient Egypt, Pythagoras, Gnostic Christianity, medieval chivalry, and Renaissance philosophy.

The 33rd degree is the one that captures the public imagination most — and is the source of endless conspiracy theorizing. In reality, it is an honorary recognition, not a repository of world-dominating secrets.

There are also the Shriners — formally the Ancient Arabic Order of the Nobles of the Mystic Shrine — who must be Master Masons to join, are famous for their fezzes and their remarkable network of Shriners Children's Hospitals, which provide free specialized medical care to children regardless of their ability to pay. The Shriners are perhaps the most publicly visible and philanthropic face of the broader Masonic family.

Famous Freemasons: The List That Explains the Obsession

Part of what fuels the Illuminati-adjacent mythology around Freemasonry is the genuinely staggering list of historically significant men who were members. When you look at who has worn the apron, it is easy to understand why people leap to conclusions about hidden control.

Among the verified Freemasons of history:

  • American Founding Fathers & Presidents: George Washington, Benjamin Franklin, Paul Revere, John Hancock, James Monroe, Andrew Jackson, James Polk, James Buchanan, Andrew Johnson, James Garfield, William McKinley, Theodore Roosevelt, William Howard Taft, Warren Harding, Franklin D. Roosevelt, Harry Truman, Gerald Ford. Fourteen American presidents in total.
  • European Figures: Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart (who encoded Masonic symbolism extensively in The Magic Flute), Joseph Haydn, Voltaire (initiated just weeks before his death), Sir Arthur Conan Doyle, Rudyard Kipling, Oscar Wilde, Sir Walter Scott, Robert Burns, Jonathan Swift, Alexander Pope, Sir Alexander Fleming (discoverer of penicillin), Edward Jenner (developer of the smallpox vaccine).
  • Military Leaders: the Duke of Wellington, General Douglas MacArthur, General John Pershing, Simon Bolivar, Giuseppe Garibaldi.
  • Royalty: King Edward VII, King George VI, King Edward VIII.

This is not a list of men on the fringes. These are the architects — in a very non-metaphorical sense — of the modern world. The concentration of Masonic membership among the Founding Fathers in particular has made the fraternity's influence on American political DNA a legitimate subject of historical inquiry, well beyond conspiracy theory.

What Freemasonry Actually Teaches

Strip away the ritual drama, the symbolic tools, the passwords, and the regalia, and the core moral teaching of Freemasonry is surprisingly straightforward. It rests on three principal tenets:

  • Brotherly Love — the practice of regarding all human beings with tolerance, respect, and kindness. Within the lodge, this means treating every brother as an equal regardless of his wealth, rank, or religion. Outside the lodge, it means extending this spirit into daily life.
  • Relief — charity, in the broadest sense. Freemasonry has an extraordinary philanthropic tradition. Masonic charities worldwide donate billions of dollars annually to hospitals, schools, disaster relief, and individual brothers and families in need. The Shriners' hospitals alone represent one of the most remarkable charitable institutions in American history.
  • Truth — the pursuit of knowledge, the practice of honesty, and the cultivation of integrity. The Masonic lodge is explicitly not a place for political debate or religious argument — its purpose is to bring men of different views together on the common ground of shared moral values.

The fraternity also teaches, through its symbolism, a profound meditation on mortality. The skull and crossbones, the coffin, the grave — these are Masonic symbols as much as pirate ones, and their purpose is entirely serious: to remind the Mason that his time is finite, that he will be judged by how he spent it, and that the building of his own character — his inner temple — is the most important work of his life.

The Church, the Conspiracy Theorists, and the Critics

Freemasonry has never been short of enemies, and they come from very different directions.

The Catholic Church

The Vatican has condemned Freemasonry repeatedly and emphatically since Pope Clement XII's bull In Eminenti in 1738 — making it one of the longest-running institutional conflicts in modern religious history. The reasons have varied over the centuries but cluster around several concerns: the secrecy of the oaths, the non-sectarian religious framework (which the Church viewed as indifferentism — the heretical suggestion that all religions are equally valid), the lodge's historical association with liberal political movements hostile to Church authority, and a general suspicion of organizations that commanded their members' primary loyalty outside the Church's structures.

As recently as 1983, the Vatican's Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith — then headed by Cardinal Joseph Ratzinger, later Pope Benedict XVI — reaffirmed that Catholics who join Masonic lodges are in a state of grave sin and may not receive communion. This remains official Church teaching today, though its enforcement at the parish level is inconsistent.

The Conspiracy Theorists

On the opposite end of the spectrum, conspiracy theorists accuse Freemasonry not of being too secular but of being too powerful — a shadow government running the world behind the scenes. The symbology on the American dollar bill (the unfinished pyramid with the all-seeing eye, captioned Annuit Coeptis and Novus Ordo Seclorum) is frequently cited as Masonic. In fact, the design was created by non-Mason Charles Thomson and largely draws on classical Roman imagery rather than Masonic symbolism — though the Eye of Providence does appear in Masonic iconography, it predates Freemasonry and belongs to a much broader Christian tradition.

The persistence of these theories is, at least partly, Freemasonry's own doing. The genuine secrecy, the rituals, the passwords, the exclusive membership, the prominent members — these are precisely the ingredients that conspiracy thinking requires. When you add the fact that Masons have historically included so many of the powerful, it is not irrational to ask whether the lodge has served as a networking mechanism for the elite. The answer to that question is almost certainly yes — but there is a significant distance between a useful professional network and a shadow government.

The Exclusion Problem

A more legitimate criticism is Freemasonry's historical record on inclusion. Traditional Freemasonry has always been male-only — women cannot join the standard lodges, though there are co-Masonic and female-only Masonic orders that most mainstream lodges do not formally recognize. More troublingly, many American lodges — particularly in the South — practiced racial exclusion well into the 20th century, refusing membership to Black men. Black Masons responded by founding their own parallel system: Prince Hall Freemasonry, established in 1784 by Prince Hall, a free Black man in Boston, after he and fourteen companions were initiated by a British military lodge. Prince Hall lodges remain a distinct and vital tradition, and mainstream Masonic recognition of them has been gradual and incomplete — a history the fraternity is still reckoning with.

Freemasonry Today

At its peak in mid-20th century America, Freemasonry had over four million members in the United States alone. Today that number has fallen to somewhere around one million, part of a broader decline in fraternal organizations, civic clubs, and face-to-face community institutions that sociologists have documented across Western societies since the 1970s.

The reasons for the decline are debated within Masonry itself: changing social habits, the loss of mystery in an information-saturated age, the time commitment required, an aging membership that has not successfully recruited younger generations. Some lodges have responded by relaxing some traditional protocols, making the experience more accessible and less arcane. Others insist that it is precisely the depth, the seriousness, and the ritual weight of the tradition that gives Freemasonry its value — and that diluting it to attract members would destroy the very thing worth joining.

Globally, Freemasonry remains a substantial presence. There are estimated to be between three and six million Masons worldwide, with lodges on every inhabited continent.

What, In the End, Is Freemasonry?

It is not a religion — it does not offer theology, sacraments, or salvation. It is not a political party — it explicitly prohibits partisan political discussion within the lodge. It is not a criminal conspiracy — its actual activities, on examination, consist primarily of ritual drama, moral philosophy, fellowship, and charity.

What it is, at its most essential, is a peculiar institution of moral education — a place where men gather, in deliberate separation from the noise and competition of the outside world, to work on themselves. The tools are symbolic. The lessons are ancient. The brotherhood is real.

Whether it has been used by powerful men to serve their own interests — almost certainly yes, as any gathering of powerful men tends to be. Whether it secretly runs the world — almost certainly no, as the world is far too chaotic to be running on anyone's schedule.

At the center of every lodge room is an altar. On it lies an open book. Above it, held in a way that frames the page, are a square and a pair of compasses. The square tests whether your foundations are true. The compasses draw the circle of your obligations — to yourself, to your brother, to your God.

In the end, that is what the handshake is really for: to recognize, in a stranger, someone who has stood at that altar, taken that obligation, and tried — however imperfectly — to build something worthy.
